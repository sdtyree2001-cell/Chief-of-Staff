I.  THE PARADOX AT THE HEART OF MEDICAL AFFAIRS INTELLIGENCE

Medical Affairs teams say they want insights that are new, novel, and actionable. But the bar they set for acting — statistical significance, sufficient N, validated data — guarantees those insights arrive too late to be any of those things. By the time a finding clears that threshold, it has been circulating long enough to generate the data required to clear it. It is, by definition, no longer new. No longer novel. And frequently no longer actionable, because the window has closed.
The bar for "knowing" something has been set so high that the field only learns what it already knew. That is not an intelligence failure. That is a methodology failure.
II.  THE WRONG RULES FOR THE WRONG ENVIRONMENT
The standard of proof Medical Affairs applies to its insights — statistical significance, peer-reviewed validation, sufficient N — is borrowed from academic science. In that environment, the standard makes sense. Publication demands it. Replication requires it. The cost of a false positive is measured in scientific credibility and patient safety, and the bar is appropriately high.
Medical Affairs does not operate in an academic environment. It operates in the wild.
In the wild — in the real-time landscape of field medicine, KOL opinion formation, competitive narrative, and patient community dialogue — speed is not a luxury. It is the variable that determines whether an insight is actionable at all. An insight that arrives at 95% confidence six months after the window has closed is not more valuable than an insight that arrives at 65% confidence while there is still time to act. It is less valuable. It is, in practice, worthless.
The consulting agencies and technology vendors that serve Medical Affairs have not challenged this mismatch. Faced with customers asking for better insights, the standard response has been a faster version of the same approach: more data, more powerful models, more sophisticated dashboards. A faster horse, when what the field needs is a car.
The AI is the paint. The methodology is the guiding hand. Pouring a larger volume of data into a more powerful model and expecting insight to emerge is not analysis — it is pattern-matching at scale. And pattern-matching at scale finds what has already happened, faster. It does not find what is about to happen.

Internal data tells you what you’ve already heard relevant to some listening priorities. One is a rear-view mirror. The other is a windshield.

V.  A DIFFERENT DEFINITION OF KNOWLEDGE
The methodology that produces true insight — new, novel, and actionable, on time — requires a different epistemological foundation. Not the justified true belief of academic science, where knowledge demands complete information and statistical warrant. Something more suited to operating in the wild: abductive reasoning. The logic of the detective, the physician at the point of care, the intelligence analyst working from incomplete evidence toward the best available explanation.
Abductive reasoning does not wait for certainty. It forms the most plausible hypothesis from available signal, acts on it provisionally, and updates as new evidence arrives. This is how Sherlock Holmes works. It is how emergency medicine works. It is how competitive intelligence works in every fast-moving industry — and it is precisely how Medical Affairs intelligence should work.
In practice, this requires four shifts:
Narrative before statistics.
Early signals arrive as stories, not p-values. A handful of patients describing the same experience in an online forum is not a statistically significant finding. It is a hypothesis. The question is not "how many people said it?" The question is "what does it mean if this is true, and what would we do differently?" Frequency is a lagging indicator. Velocity — the rate at which a narrative is spreading — is a leading one.
Hypothesis, not assertion.
An insight team that presents only validated, high-confidence findings is presenting history. An insight team that presents emerging narratives with explicit confidence levels, clear reasoning, and named assumptions is presenting intelligence. The difference is not in the data. It is in the willingness to propose before knowing — and to design field interactions that test the proposal.
The why behind the what.
Every material signal should generate a precise question for the field to take into the next KOL interaction — not a broad inquiry, but a specific, open-ended question designed to confirm or disconfirm the hypothesis. The field becomes the validation layer. Intelligence becomes a cycle, not a report.
Spin the science cycle faster.
Form a hypothesis from early signal. Test it in the field. Validate or discard. Reformulate. Launch an MVP — a minimum viable narrative — and let the market react. Fail fast. Iterate. An MA team running this cycle in weeks rather than quarters is not operating recklessly on incomplete information. It is operating provisionally on emerging information and updating continuously. That is how all good science works. It is how all good strategy works. It is the only approach fast enough to be genuinely actionable.

If that question is "how many people said it?" — that is the red flag. Not because the question is wrong, but because it is the wrong first question. Volume is the last question. The first questions are: What does this mean? What changes if it's true? What would we do differently? Who in the field can test this tomorrow?
